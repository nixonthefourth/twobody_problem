\section{Formulae}

\subsection{Planetary Position}
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{\vec{r}} = (x, y)
\end{equation}

\subsection{Distance}
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{|r|} = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Two-Body Gravitational Acceleration}
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{\vec{a}} = -\frac{GM}{|r^3|}\vec{r}
\end{equation}

Derived from Newton's Gravitational Attraction and the Law of Acceleration.

Here is Newton's Law of Gravitational Attraction:

\begin{equation}
\mathbf{\vec{F}} = G\frac{m_1 m_2}{|\vec{r}|^3}\vec{r}
\end{equation}

And here is the Second Law of Motion.

\begin{equation}
\mathbf{\vec{F}} = m\vec{a}
\end{equation}

If we combine these two, we will have an interesting derivation.

Assume $m_1$ is $M$.

Step 1:

\begin{equation}
m\vec{a} = G\frac{M m}{|\vec{r}|^3}\vec{r}
\end{equation}

Step 2:

\begin{equation}
\mathbf{\vec{a}} = G\frac{Mm}{|\vec{r^3}|m}\vec{r}
\end{equation}

The force $\vec{F}$ points to the radial vector, the gravity is attractive.

Hence why:

\begin{equation}
\mathbf{\vec{a}} = -\frac{GM}{|r^3|}\vec{r}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Energy}
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{\epsilon} = \frac{v^2}{2} - \frac{\mu}{r}
\end{equation}

However $\mu$ is switched to $M$ due to the center mass being the object system is attracted to. Hence why:

\begin{equation}
\mathbf{\epsilon} = \frac{v^2}{2} - \frac{M}{r}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Orbital Velocities}
\subsubsection{Circular}

\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Escape}

\mathbf{v_e} = \sqrt{\frac{2GM}{r}}
\end{equation}
